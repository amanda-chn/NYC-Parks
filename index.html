Understanding NYC Parks, and misleading numbers.

By Amanda Chen

NYC touts having over 30,000 acres of land, or about 16% of New York City, for its residents to enjoy. This includes more than 5,000 individual properties ranging from Coney Island Beach and Central Park to community gardens, local playgrounds, and neighborhood parks. The Trust for Public Lands rates NYC as having the 10th most effective park system in the US. The metric is based off park accessibility, acreage, amenities, and more. You can read more about it here. But, in taking a closer look at the data, I began to wonder how representative these numbers actually are.

It turns out, there are a lot of different types of lands that technically count as “park land,” falling under the jurisdiction of NYC Parks. This includes parkways, undeveloped, vacant lots, and historic properties that are typically not easily accessible to the public. With all of this in mind, a lot of NYC’s documented “park space” is actually not accessible for normal recreational use at all.

It’s no surprise Queens, Staten Island, and the Bronx, the biggest of the five boroughs, have the highest acreage of park land. But when you account for the undeveloped land, the number significantly decreases. From 30,847 acres, only about 25,021 is available for recreational use. That is a 19% difference!

In total, about 5,826 acres of NYC’s parkland aren’t accessible for public use at all. There is no doubt that green park spaces offer a great refuge from the bustling city. Years of research support the idea that nature improves human health and wellness in a multitude of different ways. Access to this land can improve mental and physical health, reduce anxiety and stress, and provide a space for safe social gatherings. New York City ranks as having the lowest green space per capita of all U.S. cities to date. With this new context in mind, the number shrinks even further to 123 sq feet of green space per NYC resident——the size of an 11 x 11 ft bedroom.
